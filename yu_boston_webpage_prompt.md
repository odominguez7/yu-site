# YU.BOSTON — Complete Webpage Build Brief

## The One-Line Brief
Build a single continuous-scroll landing page for **yu.boston** that creates visceral FOMO in investors and early-adopter employers — the kind of page where you finish scrolling and immediately reach for the CTA because you're afraid of missing the window.

---

## Design Philosophy

### Aesthetic Direction: **"Dark Editorial Intelligence"**
Think Bloomberg Terminal meets Dieter Rams meets a Gen Z zine. Not a wellness app landing page. Not pastel gradients and meditation stock photos. This is **infrastructure for human performance** — it should feel like peeking into a mission control room for something that hasn't launched yet but clearly will.

- **Dark mode dominant** — near-black backgrounds (#0A0A0B or similar), high-contrast typography
- **Monospaced accent font** for data points and stats (JetBrains Mono, IBM Plex Mono, or similar)
- **Refined serif or editorial display font** for headlines (something like Playfair Display, Fraunces, or Editorial New — not Inter, not Roboto, not Space Grotesk)
- **Clean sans-serif** for body (Söhne, Satoshi, or General Sans)
- Accent color: a single vivid tone — electric teal (#00F0D4), signal green (#00FF88), or warm amber (#FFB800) — used sparingly and deliberately. One color. Not a gradient rainbow.
- Typography-driven. Let the words do the heavy lifting. Oversized numbers. Tight kerning on headlines. Generous line height on body text.
- Subtle noise/grain texture overlay on the dark background for depth
- Micro-animations: elements fade/slide in as you scroll, but tastefully — no bouncing, no spinning, no parallax cheese

### No Sections, One Flow
The page has NO traditional section headers, no "About Us" / "Features" / "Team" boxes. It reads like a single editorial scroll — a story that builds tension and ends with a release (the CTA). Think of it as a vertical narrative, not a website with sections.

Transitions between content areas should use generous whitespace, subtle horizontal rules or fading dividers, and occasionally a full-bleed stat or quote that breaks the rhythm.

### 3. THE SHIFT (Introducing the Insight, Not the Product)

This section reframes the conversation. It's not about selling YU Shield — it's about stating a truth that makes the solution feel inevitable.

**A recent study published in _Nature Medicine_ (March 2026) demonstrated something the industry has been waiting for:** when general-purpose LLMs are augmented with a structured cognitive data layer — clinical context, behavioral history, personalized baselines — they consistently outperform standalone AI models AND human clinicians in therapeutic interactions. In a double-blind trial with 227 participants and 22 expert evaluators, augmented models delivered superior outcomes. In real-world deployment across 19,674 sessions, increased cognitive layer activation correlated with greater symptom improvement and higher likelihood of long-term clinical recovery.

**The takeaway isn't about therapy chatbots. It's about architecture:**

> When AI has access to structured personal behavioral data — not just a prompt, but a living context layer — the quality of its output transforms. The data layer IS the product.

*This is what YU Shield builds. Not another AI wellness chatbot. A behavioral intelligence layer that makes every intervention smarter, more personal, and more effective over time.*

---

### 4. WHAT YU SHIELD ACTUALLY IS (Minimal, Precise)

Don't oversell. State it clean:

**YU Shield is a B2B preventive wellbeing platform for small and mid-size businesses.**

Then three short, punchy lines (not a feature list — prose that flows):

*A 30-second daily check-in builds a personal behavioral baseline for each employee. When patterns shift — not a bad day, but sustained deviation across mood, energy, and sleep — YU delivers personalized, evidence-based micro-interventions. The employer never sees individual data. Ever. By design, not by policy.*

Then the architectural truth that creates the moat:

> **Individual data is architecturally inaccessible to employers.** Not policy-blocked. Structurally nonexistent. Employees own their data. Employers see only anonymous team aggregates. No names. No scores. No check-in notes.

> **All AI runs locally on-device.** Zero cloud. Zero data transmission. Privacy isn't a feature — it's the infrastructure.

### 5. THE MOAT (Why This Can't Be Easily Replicated)

Presented as a flowing editorial passage, not a comparison table:

*YU Shield sits in the only uncontested quadrant of the employer wellness market: proactive AND personalized. Every competitor occupies a different square. Calm and Headspace are proactive but generic. Spring Health ($3.3B valuation) and Lyra are personalized but reactive. Traditional EAPs are both reactive and generic.*

*The defensible core is the combination of four elements that are individually insufficient but together create compounding advantage:*

**Idiographic behavioral modeling** — individual baselines, not population averages. Each employee's "normal" is unique.

**Longitudinal personal data** — compounds over time. After 90 days, YU knows more about an employee's behavioral patterns than any competitor could learn on day one. Switching cost increases with every week of data.

**Clinically-designed intervention library** — every micro-intervention is CBT-informed and crafted by a licensed clinical psychologist with peer-reviewed research in digital mental health.

**Local-first AI architecture** — on-device processing via IBM Granite models. Data never leaves the machine. This isn't just a privacy feature — it's a regulatory moat for healthcare, finance, and government verticals where cloud-based wellness tools are non-starters.

### 6. SECURITY & PRIVACY (The Trust Argument)

This should feel like a manifesto, not a compliance checklist:

> **Built for the employee. Not the employer.**

*In a world where every app tracks everything, YU Shield was designed around a radical premise: the most powerful wellbeing tool is one that the employee trusts completely — because the employer literally cannot access their data.*

- On-device AI via RamaLama + IBM Granite (open-source, Apache 2.0 licensed)
- Zero cloud data transmission
- Employee-owned data with full export and deletion rights
- Minimum 5-person group threshold for any aggregate reporting
- EEOC-compliant voluntary program design
- No insurance sharing. No manager notifications without consent. No surveillance.

*This architecture was not an afterthought. It was the first design decision.*

## Technical Specifications

### Stack Recommendation
- **Single HTML file** with embedded CSS and minimal JS (for scroll animations)
- OR **React (single .jsx artifact)** with Tailwind CSS
- Scroll-triggered animations via Intersection Observer API (vanilla JS) or framer-motion (React)
- Google Fonts for typography (load 2–3 fonts max)
- No external dependencies beyond fonts
- Responsive: must look stunning on mobile and desktop
- Performance: lightweight, fast load, no heavy assets

### Scroll Behavior
- Smooth scroll snapping is optional but can add polish
- Elements animate in on scroll (fade + slight translate-y)
- Stats/numbers should animate in with a counting effect when they enter viewport
- Generous spacing between content blocks — let each statement breathe
- Consider a thin progress bar at the top of the viewport

### Color Variables (Suggested)

## Content Rules

1. **Never say "we are building a wellness app."** This is behavioral intelligence infrastructure.
2. **Never use the word "synergy," "leverage," "holistic," or "empower."**
3. **Never use stock photos.** The design is typography and data. No images needed.
4. **Don't explain what a PEPM is on the landing page.** This is an investor/early-access page, not a pricing page.
5. **Don't list features.** State truths. Let the architecture speak.
6. **The research from Nature Medicine should be referenced to frame the problem (why data layers matter for AI), not to claim YU Shield was part of that study.** YU Shield is building the same architectural principle — a cognitive data layer — for the workplace wellness context.
7. **Don't mention WhatsApp as a delivery channel** — the product has evolved beyond that. Focus on local-first AI and on-device architecture.
8. **Every stat must be real.** Sources from the investor research document: Gallup, BCG, CUNY SPH, Rock Health, SHRM, Wellhub, JMIR. Don't cite sources on the page — this isn't an academic paper. But every number must be defensible.
9. **The tone is confident but not arrogant.** Self-aware founders who know exactly what they're building and exactly what they don't know yet. The FOMO comes from inevitability — "this team, this problem, this moment" — not from hype.
10. **LinkedIn links for all three cofounders must be clickable and open in new tabs.**
